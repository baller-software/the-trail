# Product Profile — Oregon Trail

## 1. Product Overview

- **Product Name:** Oregon Trail
- **Tagline:** The original westward journey, faithful to 1971 — right in your browser.
- **Category:** Web-based retro text game
- **Current Stage:** Concept

## 2. Mission & Vision

### Mission Statement

Faithfully recreate the 1971 Oregon Trail game as a free, browser-based experience so that anyone can relive the original gameplay exactly as it was — no installs, no accounts, no cost.

### Vision Statement

Preserve a piece of early computing history by making the original Oregon Trail permanently accessible on the modern web, ensuring the game that defined a generation of classroom computing is never lost.

## 3. Target Audience

### Primary Users

- **Who they are:** Adults (30–60+) who played the original Oregon Trail in school during the 1970s–1990s and remember it fondly.
- **Key pain points:** The original game is difficult to access — it required specific hardware, emulators, or outdated software. Modern remakes often change the gameplay beyond recognition.
- **Goals and motivations:** Relive the nostalgia of the original experience. Share the game with their children or friends. Satisfy curiosity about whether the game holds up.

### Secondary Users

- **Retro computing enthusiasts** who appreciate historically accurate software preservation, even if they didn't play the original.
- **Educators and students** interested in the historical context of both the Oregon Trail itself and early educational software.

### Stakeholders

Not applicable — this is an independent passion project with no external stakeholders, buyers, or partners.

## 4. Value Proposition

### Core Value

An authentic, zero-friction way to play the original 1971 Oregon Trail game. No downloads, no emulators, no paywalls — just open a browser and play.

### Key Benefits

1. **Authentic experience** — Faithful to the original 1971 gameplay mechanics, text-based interface, and green/amber monochrome aesthetic.
2. **Instant access** — Runs in any modern web browser with no installation or account required.
3. **Completely free** — No ads, no microtransactions, no strings attached.
4. **Preservation** — Keeps an important piece of computing and educational history alive and accessible.

### Differentiation

- Most Oregon Trail remakes modernize the visuals and mechanics significantly. This project prioritizes historical accuracy over modern game design.
- Unlike emulator-based approaches, this runs natively in the browser with no setup.
- No commercial motives means no compromise between authenticity and monetization.

## 5. Product Description

### What It Is

A browser-based recreation of the 1971 Oregon Trail game originally written by Don Rawitsch, Bill Heinemann, and Paul Dillenberger. The player assumes the role of a wagon leader guiding a party of settlers from Independence, Missouri to Oregon's Willamette Valley in 1848. The game is played entirely through text — the player makes decisions about supplies, pace, and route while managing random events like disease, river crossings, broken equipment, and hunting.

The interface replicates the monochrome terminal aesthetic of the era — green or amber text on a black background, fixed-width fonts, and minimal graphical embellishment. Gameplay follows the original's structure: buy supplies, travel in turns, respond to events, and try to survive the 2,000-mile journey.

### What It Isn't

- **Not a modern remake** — This is not a reimagining with pixel art, animations, or contemporary game design patterns.
- **Not a mobile app** — While it may be playable on mobile browsers, the experience is designed for desktop.
- **Not an educational platform** — While the original had educational roots, this project focuses on gameplay recreation, not curriculum integration.
- **Not multiplayer** — This is a single-player experience, just like the original.

## 6. Use Cases & Scenarios

### Primary Use Cases

1. **Nostalgia replay** — A player who remembers the game from childhood opens the site, starts a game, and experiences the same decision-making and random events they remember.
2. **Sharing the experience** — A parent sends the link to their kid and says "this is what we played in school." The kid can start playing immediately with no setup.
3. **Quick session gaming** — A player has 15–30 minutes and wants a self-contained, complete gaming experience with a clear beginning and end.
4. **Historical curiosity** — Someone who has heard about the Oregon Trail game but never played it tries it for the first time to understand what the cultural phenomenon was about.

## 7. Market & Competition

### Market Context

Retro gaming and software preservation are active communities. The Oregon Trail holds an outsized place in American cultural memory — it is one of the most recognized educational games ever made. There is steady, low-volume but persistent interest in playing or revisiting the game, driven by nostalgia and cultural references.

### Competitive Landscape

- **Direct competitors:** Internet Archive hosts playable versions of various Oregon Trail editions via emulator. The official "The Oregon Trail" franchise (Gameloft/Houghton Mifflin Harcourt) has released modernized commercial versions.
- **Indirect competitors:** Other retro game preservation projects, browser-based classic game collections, and Oregon Trail parody/tribute games.
- **White space:** There is no widely-known, purpose-built browser recreation of the *1971 original* that prioritizes authentic gameplay and aesthetic over modernization. Most available versions are either later editions (1985, 1990s) or heavily modernized remakes.

## 13. Risks & Assumptions

### Key Assumptions

- The original 1971 game's mechanics are sufficiently documented (through source code, articles, and community knowledge) to enable an accurate recreation.
- A browser-based text interface can faithfully replicate the feel of a 1970s terminal.
- The target audience values authenticity over modern convenience.

### Risks

- **Intellectual property** — The Oregon Trail name and game have been commercially licensed. A recreation must be careful about trademark and copyright considerations.
- **Accuracy** — Without access to the exact original source code, some gameplay mechanics may need to be approximated.
- **Limited appeal** — A strictly authentic 1971 recreation may feel too sparse or dated for players expecting more polish, even nostalgic ones.

### Mitigation Strategies

- Research IP status thoroughly. Consider using a distinct project name if trademark issues arise. Focus on recreating mechanics rather than copying copyrighted assets.
- Cross-reference multiple historical sources (published source code listings, academic papers, contemporary accounts) to maximize accuracy.
- Set clear expectations in the product's messaging — this is a preservation project, not a modern game.
